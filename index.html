Models as Candidate Learners

A linguist's tour of developmental interpretability
Lab Meeting · Week 2 Spring 2026

Learners Learn in Stages

The central observation of acquisition research:

  • Past-tense morphology. A child learns went, then overgeneralizes to goed, then recovers to went again — a U-shaped curve. The trickier part: they retreat from the overgeneralization without anyone telling them they were wrong. No explicit negative evidence.
  • Wh-questions. Children pass through ordered stages — from declarative-form questions to partial inversion to full subject-aux inversion — not gradual improvement.

Stages, not smooth improvement. Sometimes non-monotonic. Decades of acquisition research has documented this as the shape of learning.

And So Do Neural Networks

Three phenomena from machine learning that look strikingly familiar:

  • Grokking — a model memorizes, then suddenly generalizes thousands of epochs later
  • Induction heads — in-context learning appears abruptly mid-training
  • Emergent abilities — capabilities absent in smaller models appear suddenly at scale

Loss plateaus, then drops. Structure appears suddenly, not incrementally — and sometimes non-monotonically.

Same observation, different system. The shape of learning rhymes with what acquisition researchers have been describing for decades.

The Bet

If models are candidate learners — traversing the same kind of stagewise developmental landscape we see in kids — then something striking follows:

  • They're the first learners we can fully open up. Every weight, every loss, every checkpoint is observable in a way no human learner ever will be.
  • That's a methodological gift the study of acquisition has never had.

But to take the bet seriously, we need a framework that lets us treat "stages" in a model rigorously — when one starts, when it ends, what changes between them. Classical learning theory can't give us one.

The Classical Promise

Classical statistics demands regular models:

  • The map from parameters to functions is one-to-one — every parameter configuration gives a unique output
  • The Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) is positive definite everywhere — the loss landscape is a smooth, convex bowl

Under these conditions, the Bernstein–von Mises theorem guarantees the posterior concentrates around a single unique minimum as data increases .

Regular model geometry

A marble rolling to the bottom of a perfectly smooth bowl. More data = tighter convergence.

Why Neural Networks Break This

Neural networks violate both requirements of regular models:

  • Permutation symmetry — swap two neurons (with their weights), same function. Millions of parameter configs → one output.
  • Dead neurons — a ReLU stuck at zero lets you scale its weights arbitrarily. Parameter space is unmoored from function space.
  • Fisher Information goes singular — zero eigenvalues, FIM no longer invertible, Bernstein–von Mises shatters.

The loss landscape is not a bowl — it's a sprawling valley system where wildly different parameters share the exact same loss.

The Toy Model of Superposition

A single-hidden-layer autoencoder :

  • 6 features compressed into 2 hidden dimensions — a forced bottleneck
  • Encode: $h = Wx$, decode: $f(x) = \text{ReLU}(W^T h + b)$
  • Goal: reconstruct $x$ from the bottleneck — but 6 features can't fit in 2 dims without overlap

The model must choose: which features get faithful representation, and which get compressed into superposition?

Singular Learning Theory

Watanabe (2009): import algebraic geometry to handle singularities . The key tool: the Local Learning Coefficient (LLC, $\hat\lambda$)

  • $\lambda$ = half the number of "directions that matter" — directions where changing parameters actually changes the loss
  • Low LLC = broad flat basin, simple structure, fewer features committed
  • High LLC = narrow sharp basin, complex structure, more features captured

Simple basins are occupied first (geometrically cheaper). The model transitions to more complex ones only when the free energy trade-off tips .

Interactive: Loss Landscape

Free Energy: The Governing Equation

$$F = \underbrace{L_n(w)}_{\text{empirical loss}} + \underbrace{\lambda(w) \cdot \frac{\log n}{n}}_{\text{complexity penalty}}$$

$\lambda(w)$ is the LLC, $n$ is the effective sample size (≈ training time).

  • Early training ($n$ small): complexity penalty dominates → biased toward simple basins
  • Late training ($n$ large): penalty shrinks → can afford more complex solutions if data demands it

The model's preferred basin changes over training. Not just minimizing loss — performing Bayesian internal model selection.

What Did Chen et al. Find?

Investigated: In a toy model where the loss landscape is fully analytically tractable, how do phase transitions actually work — and does Bayesian model selection (the theory) match what SGD does (the practice)?

Found:

  • The superposition model's loss landscape decomposes into discrete k-gon basins, each with a distinct LLC
  • Free energy $F = L_n + \lambda \cdot \frac{\log n}{n}$ governs which basin is preferred — early training favors low-$\lambda$ (simple), late training tolerates high-$\lambda$ (complex) only if the data demands it
  • Both Bayesian sampling and SGD exhibit discrete, stepwise transitions between basins — and in the same order
  • The signature: opposing staircases — loss drops in steps while LLC rises in steps

Tells us: SLT doesn't just describe singular models in the abstract — it makes concrete, observable predictions about how learning unfolds. Even SGD, which has nothing to do with Bayesian sampling, follows the free-energy script.

Three Predictions for Real Models

If Chen et al. is right that SLT governs real learning — not just an analytically tractable toy — then in a real transformer trained on real data, we should see:

  • 1 Discrete LLC jumps, not smooth growth — training proceeds in stages, and the LLC marks where they begin and end
  • 2 Jumps aligned with new capabilities — each LLC transition should correspond to something the model can newly do
  • 3 The opposing-staircase signature — loss falls stepwise as LLC rises stepwise, just like the toy model

These are testable. To test them, we need a way to measure the LLC in models too large to enumerate — and a behavioral framework for asking whether each transition means anything.

Hoogland et al. (2024) set out to do exactly this.

Scaling to Richer Environments

The toy superposition model has a clean, analytically tractable loss landscape. Do the same stagewise dynamics appear in more complex training environments?

In-Context Regression

Transient Structures & the Ridge→DMMSE Transition

The Task

Training on In-Context Regression

The transformer sees (x, y) pairs from a noisy linear function $y = t^\top x + \varepsilon$ and must predict each $y_i$ from preceding context. Per-token loss drops in stages — each corresponding to a new regression strategy.

Interactive: In-Context Regression Task

Opposing Staircases in Regression

Loss shows a large initial drop (LR1–LR2), then steady decrease through LR3–LR5.

LLC tracks loss early — complexity rises as loss falls (LR1–LR3). The model is building structure to solve the task.

But in LR4–LR5, LLC decreases while loss continues to fall. The model is shedding complexity — simplifying its internal algorithm while still improving.

Opposite direction from the toy model — the signature of a transient structure being replaced.

LR1: Position-Invariant Predictions

(a) Per-token prediction norms $\mathbb{E}[\|\hat{y}_k\|^2]$ across context positions $k$.

Initially, the model predicts zero at all positions — the safest prior when you know nothing about the task.

As training progresses, predictions grow, but remain position-invariant early on: the model hasn't yet learned that later positions (more context) should produce better predictions.

LR2–3: In-Context Learning Emerges

(b) In-context learning score across training, evaluated at different levels of distributional shift ($g$).

$g = 1$ is the training distribution. Higher $g$ = inputs drawn from a wider distribution — testing out-of-distribution generalization.

In LR2, ICL performance improves across all $g$ values — the model learns a general regression algorithm that transfers to unseen scales.

This is the ridge regression phase — a simple, robust algorithm that generalizes broadly.

LR4–5: Specialization Costs Generalization

As the model loses complexity (LLC drops in LR4–5), it begins to lose out-of-distribution ICL performance as well.

Higher $g$ values degrade first — the model is specializing to the training distribution at the expense of general-purpose regression.

The transient ridge solution is being dismantled and replaced by a narrower algorithm tuned to the specific training data.

Layer-Norm Collapse

(c) Layer-norm weights over training.

In LR4–5, layer-norm weights collapse toward zero for many dimensions.

This directly corresponds to the LLC decrease — fewer active dimensions means fewer "directions that matter" in parameter space.

The model is effectively pruning its own capacity, trading the general-purpose ridge solution for a specialized one with fewer degrees of freedom.

Embedding Collapse

During LR4–5, the token embeddings reorganize:

  • (a) Token embedding singular values collapse — the orthogonal $y$ representation merges into the input embedding space
  • (b) Positional encoding similarly compresses — position-specific structure is shed
  • (c) Cosine similarity between $W_{\text{embed}}$ and $W_{\text{postn}}$ rises — subspaces align into a simpler joint representation

Orthogonality traded for compression: tighter fit to training, less OOD capacity.

What Did Hoogland et al. Find?

Investigated: Whether SLT-predicted phase transitions correspond to observable changes in model behavior — in both language modeling and in-context regression.

Found:

  • Training proceeds in discrete stages, identifiable by LLC dynamics, in both settings
  • In LMs: stages align with bigrams → positional awareness → previous token heads → induction heads
  • In regression: the model builds a general-purpose algorithm (ridge), then dismantles it in favor of a specialized one — a transient structure
  • LLC can decrease during training (LR4–5), contradicting the toy model prediction — the theory needs to account for this

Tells us: Development is real, observable, and structured. The LLC is a reliable signal for when to look — the behavioral probes tell you what happened.

A Recipe for Developmental Analysis

  • 1Pick a theoretically grounded measure. Not loss, not accuracy — something that captures structural properties. The LLC comes from SLT, but the principle extends: choose a measure with foundations that tells you what the loss curve can't.
  • 2Track it developmentally. Measure throughout training at sufficient density. Log-spaced checkpoints — development is faster early, slower late.
  • 3Let the measure define the stages. Don't impose stages from behavioral observations. Let the measure's own dynamics (LLC derivative zero-crossings) partition training. Unsupervised, setting-agnostic.
  • 4Validate with behavioral probes. Now look at what changed at each boundary. Design probes, run ablations. The measure told you when. The analysis tells you what.
  • 5Interrogate the gap between theory and data. The theory predicted LLC-increasing transitions. The data showed LLC-decreasing ones too. That mismatch is the most valuable finding — it's where the theory needs to grow.

A Linguist's Note

The regression results demonstrate something striking from a language acquisition perspective:

The model is effectively solving the subset problem — even if maladaptively. It finds a general solution first, then without negative evidence, free-energy dynamics and optimizer noise push it out of a superset solution and into a subset of the problem.

The trajectory — zero → generalize → sub-generalize — mirrors a Chomskyan approach to parameter setting, which posits that learners progress through a none → all → some pattern.

This is a general learner navigating its way out of the poverty-of-the-stimulus problem — free energy dynamics and optimizer noise provide the mechanism to escape overly general minima, without requiring negative evidence. The geometry of the hypothesis space does the work.

Testing the Story on Two Models

Chen et al. and Hoogland et al. give us a complete picture of when transitions happen and how complex the model becomes. The LLC marks the boundaries; the Recipe tells us how to act on them.

What's still missing: which data drives the transitions? Lee et al. take this question to two settings:

  • A controlled toy model — Saxe et al.'s deep linear network learning hierarchical concepts. Analytical ground truth, known developmental stages, a clean story we can unpack end-to-end.
  • A real language model — Pythia 14M on The Pile, the same setting Hoogland and Tigges studied. Does the framework survive contact with a real transformer?

If influence dynamics align with the LLC-detected transitions, we have a unified picture: the LLC tells us when, the BIF tells us which.

Toy Model

Hierarchical Concept Learning

The Saxe Toy Model

From Saxe, McClelland & Ganguli 2019: a 2-layer deep linear neural network that learns to map item identity (a one-hot vector) to a vector of features .

  • The dataset has hierarchical structure — items inherit features from their categories at multiple levels (living → plant/animal → tree/flower/bird/mammal → individual)
  • Analytically tractable — the learning dynamics have a closed-form solution in terms of the data covariance's singular value decomposition
  • A known developmental story — Saxe showed the network learns categorical distinctions in stages, ordered from coarsest to finest, via singular-mode-by-singular-mode emergence

Concretely, what does the data and the network look like?

The Toy Model: Hierarchical Concepts

What Saxe Already Told Us

The data has a clear similarity structure — because items inherit features hierarchically, the item-by-item similarity matrix shows nested blocks (animals vs plants, then mammals vs birds, ...).

Saxe et al. showed the network learns these distinctions in stages as loss decreases — first the coarsest split, then progressively finer ones, ending at item-level differentiation .

This tells us when categorical distinctions emerge. But it doesn't tell us about the dynamics between items at each stage — which items are competing for representational room, which are being held together. That's what BIF can show us.

Why Not Classical Influence Functions?

The classical influence function hands you one number per (sample, observable) pair, computed at the end of training:

$$\text{IF}(z_i, \phi) \;=\; -\,\underbrace{\nabla\phi(w^*)^\top}_{\text{gradient of observable}}\;\underbrace{H^{-1}(w^*)}_{\text{inverse Hessian}}\;\underbrace{\nabla\ell_i(w^*)}_{\text{loss gradient of sample } z_i}$$
  • The inverse Hessian doesn't exist in singular models — the same Hessian-blindness we hit in the Hoogland section
  • The single number assumes influence is static — but in stagewise learning, a sample's role changes over training

We need something Hessian-free and defined throughout training.

Bayesian Influence Functions: The Intuition

At any checkpoint, ask a simple question: if the weights were slightly different, would dog's loss and cat's loss move in the same direction?

SGLD samples nearby weight configurations. At each, you measure both losses. Their covariance across the sample is the BIF .

No Hessian inverse. Well-defined at any checkpoint. And mathematically, it's a susceptibility — the kind of quantity that diverges at phase transitions.

How the BIF Is Computed, Mechanically

Two Predictions for Influence Dynamics

If influence is a susceptibility, then at a phase transition where the posterior splits between two phases $\mathcal{U}$ and $\mathcal{V}$, decomposing the BIF via the law of total covariance gives two concrete predictions:

  • 1Influence can flip sign. The "within-phase" influence may differ between $\mathcal{U}$ and $\mathcal{V}$ — so a sample that helps learning before a transition can hurt learning after, or vice versa.
  • 2Influence peaks at transitions. The "between-phase" term is maximized when posterior mass is split 50/50, and its magnitude scales with how strongly the two phases disagree on the sample. Peaks identify the samples that characterize a given transition.

These are the signatures to look for as we walk through the toy model dynamics.

Sign Flips Track the Hierarchy

  • Sign flips happen — pairs reverse their influence relationship at each developmental boundary
  • Peaks line up with stages — maximum influence between two items occurs exactly when the boundary between them is forming
  • BIF agrees with LOO retraining — the rightmost panel reproduces the dynamics from a full leave-one-out training sweep

Stage 0: A Single Animacy Axis

  • One low-dimensional explanation captures every item — roughly, is_living
  • No category splits yet; influence between items is small and uniform
  • Nothing yet for developmental pressure to push against

Stage 1: Animal vs. Plant

  • With dog as reference, lily sits in developmental pressure — both susceptible, both deserve to differ
  • Cat sits in developmental relief — covary, held together while the model anticipates the category split
  • Split resolves dog–lily pressure; cat–dog relief persists (same category, no need to differentiate yet)

Stage 2: Mammal vs. Bird

  • Initially the animal split is enough — parrot and dog co-exist on the same side
  • Parrot enters negative covariance with dog → pressure on the mammal/bird boundary
  • Model splits along mammal/bird; cat–dog relief still persists

Stage 3: Each Item Alone

  • Cat–dog relief finally flips into pressure — the covariant pocket has become a strained gradient
  • Model splits the parameter space one last time
  • Each item now in its own gradient space — no more inter-item influence; the developmental story is complete

Does It Scale to a Real Transformer?

The toy model showed BIF tracking exactly the developmental story Saxe predicted. The harder test: does the same machinery work on a real language model trained on real text?

  • Test bed: Pythia-14M , trained on The Pile (~300B tokens). Use the publicly released checkpoints — log-spaced from step 1 out to ~55k.
  • Estimation: SGLD with 4 chains × 200 steps from each checkpoint. Use the normalized BIF (Pearson correlation) for stability across checkpoints with very different loss scales.
  • The prediction to test: induction-circuit formation. Tigges et al. 2024 placed it around step 30k via mechanistic interpretability — does BIF independently see the same thing?

Causal Intervention: When Matters

A 3M-parameter model trained six times with identical initialization and data ordering — only the timing of induction-pattern token upweighting (4×, in a 500-step window) differs. Same data, different timing.

BIF dynamics on the unweighted baseline predict the effect. Upweighting before the BIF transition (when influence is flat or rising) delays or disrupts induction-circuit formation. Upweighting during the BIF descent accelerates it — heads form as early as step 5k versus ~7k in the baseline.

What the LM Section Showed

  • BIF independently flags the induction window. The trajectory inflects around step 128 and resolves by ~30k — agreeing with what mechanistic interpretability had already found through circuit analysis (Tigges et al. 2024).
  • The intervention experiment is the strongest result. BIF dynamics on the baseline predict whether upweighting will accelerate or disrupt circuit formation. Same data, different timing, opposite effect.
  • The framework transfers from controlled toy model to real transformer — at least for the cleanest case. But the LM section also raises questions worth taking seriously.

Limitations: How Much Should We Trust the LM Results?

  • Wrong scale. 14M parameters on ~300B tokens is a jammed regime — the $nL$ term dominates the free energy, leaving no complexity budget for sharp phase transitions. Many features may stay in permanent superposition: relief without consolidation.
  • Gradual, not sharp. The toy-model BIFs sign-flip and peak unambiguously. The LM BIFs are smooth trends with at-best ambiguous inflections. Real gradual development, or a capacity artifact? One run can't tell us.
  • Construct validity of "induction tokens." The class is operationalized as any bigram that has already occurred in the context window. In natural English, that means it's dominated by mundane function-word repetitions ("of the," "in the," "to the") that the model can already predict from base rates — not the linguistically interesting in-context copying we'd want it to track. The aggregate signal survives because an induction circuit helps with all of these, but the classification doesn't carry the linguistic theory we'd want for finer claims.
  • Needs Chen-style scaling analysis. Vary model size and data size to actually test the theory's scale-generality. The current results validate a single point on that diagram.

Future Directions: A Linguistic Methodology Paper

The natural application: take this framework to a real question about syntactic acquisition. BabyLM (~100M words, English-only) is small enough that dense checkpointing and leave-one-out training are computationally feasible — and it already has the infrastructure we'd need:

  • BLiMP gives 67 syntactic minimal-pair subtasks for free as evaluation observables — loss on grammatical vs. ungrammatical members of each pair is a clean signal
  • The CHILDES portion of BabyLM already has rich morphosyntactic annotation, so the "annotated training corpus" side is partially built
  • The BabyLM community is already asking which data sources contribute to syntactic knowledge and does order of exposure matter — both of which this framework can answer

Connection to my own work: I do controlled rearing studies — leave-one-out at the corpus level — to investigate the learning contribution of specific linguistic evidence. BIF would give the forward-exploratory tool to identify where to look before committing to expensive retraining runs. The two methods are complementary.

A Three-Layer Pipeline (One Per Paper)

  • Layer 1 — LLC dynamics (Chen, Hoogland). Find the developmental map: where the transitions are, how many, when. Pure model-side, no linguistic interpretation yet — but it gives the timestamps to anchor everything else.
  • Layer 2 — BIF on syntactic tasks (Lee). Take BLiMP minimal pairs as the evaluation observable. At each LLC-identified stage, compute BIF between annotated training-data features (POS tags, dependency relations, morphology, ...) and each syntactic construct. Tells us which data drives which acquisition.
  • Layer 3 — Targeted controlled rearing (Lee, Appendix D.4). Take the strongest BIF findings and test causally: remove the implicated training tokens during the specific window where BIF says they matter, and check whether the acquisition of that construct is delayed or disrupted. Same logic as the induction-upweighting experiment, applied to a linguistic question.

The contribution isn't a new linguistic finding — it's a toolkit. Other researchers can apply the same pipeline to their own constructions of interest.

The Path We've Traced

We started with a problem: classical learning theory can't explain why neural networks learn in jumps. We finished with a methodology that can — and the beginnings of one that can intervene on it.

  • Chen et al. showed that singular models — like neural networks — learn through discrete phase transitions, governed by a free-energy tradeoff between fit and complexity. The toy model is a clean proof of concept.
  • Hoogland et al. showed the same dynamics appear in real transformers. The LLC is a reliable signal for when a transition happens; behavioral probes tell us what.
  • Lee et al. closed the loop. The BIF tells us which data drives each transition — and the intervention experiment showed that knowing this lets you causally shape the developmental trajectory.

Developmental interpretability has gone from describing stagewise learning to intervening on it. The next question is how to take that machinery to the questions we care about as linguists.
